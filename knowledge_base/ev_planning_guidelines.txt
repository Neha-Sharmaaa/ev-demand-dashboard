EV INFRASTRUCTURE PLANNING GUIDELINES
======================================
Source: Urban EV Infrastructure Best Practices (2024)

SECTION 1: CHARGER DENSITY STANDARDS
Recommended charger density is 1 Level 2 charger per 20 registered EVs in a zone.
For high-demand urban zones exceeding 50 kWh average hourly load, deploy at minimum
1 DC Fast Charger (50 kW) per 500 EVs. Mixed fleets require a ratio of 70% AC Level 2
to 30% DC Fast. Rural zones may reduce density to 1 charger per 40 EVs. Charger
clusters of 4 to 8 units improve grid efficiency and reduce cabling costs by 30%.

SECTION 2: GRID CAPACITY THRESHOLDS
A zone's peak charging demand must not exceed 80% of the substation's rated capacity.
Zones with average demand above 30 kWh per hour require dedicated transformer upgrades.
Grid connection should be sized at 1.3x peak load to accommodate demand growth over
5 years. Three-phase supply is mandatory for DC Fast Chargers rated above 22 kW.
Load factor should remain below 0.75 for residential zones and 0.85 for commercial zones.

SECTION 3: PEAK LOAD MANAGEMENT
Smart charging systems must implement time-of-use (TOU) pricing to shift load away
from peak hours (typically 17:00 to 21:00). Dynamic load balancing reduces peak draw
by 20 to 35%. On-site battery energy storage (BESS) of at least 100 kWh is recommended
for zones with peak demand exceeding 150 kWh. Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) systems can
reduce net peak load by up to 25% in high-density residential zones.

SECTION 4: ZONING AND REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS
EV charger installations must comply with local building codes and NEC Article 625.
Minimum setback from property boundaries is 1.5 meters. Accessibility compliance
(ADA) mandates one accessible charger per 25 installed units. Environmental impact
assessments are required for installations above 500 kW capacity. Permits typically
require 30 to 90 days; fast-track permitting programs available in 38 states.

SECTION 5: DEMAND RESPONSE INTEGRATION
Zones should enroll in utility demand response programs to receive incentives for
curtailing charging during grid stress events. Automated demand response (ADR) systems
reduce peak demand charges by 15 to 25%. Aggregators can pool distributed EV loads
to participate in wholesale electricity markets. Forecast-based pre-charging strategies
reduce morning peak demand by scheduling overnight charging more efficiently.

SECTION 6: CHARGER TYPE SELECTION CRITERIA
Level 1 (1.4 kW): Suitable for overnight residential with 8 to 12 hour dwell times.
Level 2 AC (7.2 kW to 22 kW): Optimal for workplace, retail, and public parking.
DC Fast Charger (50 kW to 350 kW): Required for highway corridors and high-turnover
commercial sites. Ultra-fast chargers (150 kW+) needed when average dwell time is
under 20 minutes. Bidirectional chargers support V2G but cost 2 to 3x standard units.

SECTION 7: SAFETY AND RELIABILITY STANDARDS
All chargers must be UL Listed and comply with SAE J1772, CHAdeMO, or CCS standards.
Ground fault circuit interrupter (GFCI) protection is mandatory. Surge protection
devices rated for the local lightning exposure category are required. Charger uptime
SLA of 97% is the industry standard for public infrastructure. Redundant communication
systems (4G/LTE + Wi-Fi) ensure continuous payment and monitoring capability.

SECTION 8: FINANCIAL BENCHMARKS AND ROI
Average cost per Level 2 port including installation: $3,000 to $7,500.
DC Fast Charger installed cost: $30,000 to $80,000.
Expected payback period for high-traffic commercial sites: 3 to 5 years.
Federal tax credit (30C) covers 30% of equipment and installation costs up to $100,000.
Utility incentive programs offset 10 to 40% of infrastructure costs in most states.
Revenue per charger averages $8,000 to $25,000 annually at high-utilization sites.

SECTION 9: SITE SELECTION CRITERIA
Priority sites include retail centers (dwell time 45-90 min), workplaces (6-9 hours),
multifamily housing (8-12 hours overnight), and transit hubs (20-60 min). Sites must
have existing electrical service of at least 200A single phase or 100A three phase.
Proximity to distribution transformer (within 50 meters) reduces installation cost
significantly. Avoid sites with poor cellular coverage unless fiber backhaul available.

SECTION 10: FUTURE-PROOFING AND SCALABILITY
Install conduit and panel capacity for 2x projected charger count to support expansion.
Specify "charger-ready" infrastructure (conduit, wiring, panel) before full deployment.
Select networked chargers with open standards (OCPP 1.6/2.0) to avoid vendor lock-in.
Plan for 10% annual demand growth in urban zones and 20% in high-growth suburban zones.
Microgrids with solar-plus-storage reduce long-term utility dependency by 30 to 50%.
